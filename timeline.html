TIMELINE

Introduction

Establishment of the First Philippine Republic
(June 12, 1898)

The establishment of the First Philippine Republic marked a historic moment in the struggle for Philippine independence. On June 12, 1898, in Kawit, Cavite, Filipino revolutionary leaders, led by Emilio Aguinaldo, declared the Philippines as an independent and sovereign nation, ending over three centuries of Spanish colonial rule.

Introduction

Malolos Congress and Adoption of the
Malolos Constitution (January 23, 1899)

Following the proclamation of independence, the Malolos Congress convened to draft and adopt a constitution for the newly established republic. On January 23, 1899, the Malolos Constitution was ratified, laying the foundation for the governance of the First Philippine Republic and enshrining principles of democracy and sovereignty.

Introduction

Outbreak of the Philippine-American War
(February 4, 1899)

The outbreak of the Philippine-American War on February 4, 1899, marked a tumultuous period in Philippine history. The war, which lasted until 1902, was fought between Filipino nationalists seeking independence and American colonial forces. It had a profound impact on the fate of the First Philippine Republic and shaped the course of Philippine-American relations.

Introduction

Capture of Emilio Aguinaldo
(March 23, 1901)

On March 23, 1901, Emilio Aguinaldo, the president of the First Philippine Republic, was captured by American forces led by General Frederick Funston. Aguinaldo's capture dealt a significant blow to the republic's leadership and signaled the beginning of the end of organized resistance against American rule.

Introduction

End of Organized Resistance
(April 1, 1901)

The formal surrender of Filipino forces and the end of organized resistance to American rule on April 1, 1901, marked the conclusion of the Philippine-American War. With the defeat of Filipino forces, the First Philippine Republic ceased to exist as an independent entity, and the Philippines came under American colonial rule.

Legacy & Impact

Establishment of the First Philippine Republic
(June 12, 1898)

The establishment of the First Philippine Republic left a lasting legacy as the first sovereign Filipino government. It symbolized the culmination of decades of struggle against Spanish colonial rule and served as a beacon of hope for Filipino nationalists fighting for independence. The republic's declaration of sovereignty laid the foundation for Filipino nationhood and inspired future generations to continue the quest for self-determination.

Legacy & Impact

Malolos Congress and Adoption of the
Malolos Constitution (January 23, 1899)

The adoption of the Malolos Constitution was a significant milestone in the history of Philippine governance. It represented a bold experiment in democratic nation-building, laying out principles of liberty, equality, and sovereignty. Despite its eventual suspension following the American occupation, the Malolos Constitution provided a blueprint for future Philippine constitutions and contributed to the development of constitutional democracy in the country.

Legacy & Impact

Outbreak of the Philippine-American War
(February 4, 1899)

The Philippine-American War had a profound impact on the trajectory of Philippine history. While the war resulted in the eventual colonization of the Philippines by the United States, it also galvanized Filipino nationalism and resistance against foreign domination. The sacrifices made by Filipino soldiers and civilians during the war helped forge a sense of national identity and unity, laying the groundwork for future struggles for independence.

Legacy & Impact

Capture of Emilio Aguinaldo
(March 23, 1901)

The capture of Emilio Aguinaldo by American forces dealt a significant blow to the First Philippine Republic and marked the effective end of organized armed resistance against American rule. Aguinaldo's surrender led to the dismantling of the republic's government and the consolidation of American colonial control over the Philippines. However, Aguinaldo's legacy as a nationalist leader continued to inspire future generations of Filipinos in their quest for freedom and independence.

Legacy & Impact

End of Organized Resistance
(April 1, 1901)

The formal surrender of Filipino forces marked the beginning of a new chapter in Philippine history under American colonial rule. While the end of organized resistance represented a setback for the First Philippine Republic, it also paved the way for the eventual transition to civilian governance and the establishment of institutions that would shape the future trajectory of the Philippines. Despite the challenges posed by colonization, the legacy of the First Philippine Republic endured as a testament to the resilience and determination of the Filipino people in their pursuit of freedom and self-determination.
